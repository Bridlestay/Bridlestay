

## 1. Overall Philosophy: Community, Not Gamification. Not in the way they think.

The core decision is to **avoid hard gamification entirely**:

- No points, levels, leaderboards, or competition
- No pressure to perform, rank, or “win”
- No social rank between profiles

Instead, the platform uses:

- **Editorial curation**
- **Light rituals**
- **Structured contribution**
- **Quiet social validation**    

This suits:

- An older, practical audience
- Horse people who value usefulness, trust, and stewardship
- A premium, calm, considered brand

Everything is framed as _helping the next rider_, not generating content. It avoids seeming desperate or hungry for more. It feeds the philosophy of engaging with an underserved community and elevating them through the platform.


## 2. Editorial Highlight (Front Page Pull)

A rotating, curated section on the homepage acts as the **primary hook**, immediately showing:

- Routes are being ridden
- People are engaging
- The ecosystem is alive

This section:

- Features routes with recent rides, comments, and photos
- Shows human signals (e.g. “Ridden 14 times”, “Often chosen by guests nearby”)
- Uses evocative photos (moments, not hero shots)

It feels **editorial, not algorithmic**, and encourages exploration of:

- Other routes
- Nearby stays

This is the strongest signal that Bridlestay is a _living network_ of rides and places to stay, despite it all being an automated feature based on tagging and algorithms. This will also instil patterned behaviour from our users and encourages content we desire to see.


## 3. Route Engagement: “I’ve Ridden This”

Because this is a website (not an app), **real-time GPS tracking is not relied upon**.

The keystone interaction is a single, dignified action:

> **“I’ve ridden this route”**

This:

- Requires no proof
- Matches real-world language
- Feels natural to older users

Clicking it:

- Increments a ride count
- Unlocks the review flow of cards
- Enables photo uploads
- Feeds editorial highlights throughout the website

No timings, stats, or competition are required. Bare in mind, a lot of old people need some serious hand holding with obvious, clean and minimal content. AirBnB often employs very basic design for pushing users through their service they way they want, for little distraction.


## 4. Review System: Short, Structured and Led

The review flow is **card-based, linear, and intentionally short** to maximise completion. Especially from the not-so-tech-savvy and older users. Horse people will not ut up with much horsing around. Neigh way.

### Core review steps:

1. **Overall feel**

    - Star rating collected, but not publicly displayed
    - Framed as “How did this route ride overall?”
        
2. **Add a moment (optional photo)**

    - Prompted as sharing a memory, not uploading content
    - Suggested categories (view, feature, seasonal moment)
        
3. **Things worth knowing (checkboxes)**  

    This is the most valuable part:
    - Muddy after rain
    - Road crossings
    - Suitable for steady / experienced horses
    - Water available
    - Group friendly, etc.

Checkboxes:

- Are quick
- Feel helpful    
- Produce structured, reusable data
- Introduce easy tagging data for us to use site-wide

4. **Optional short note**
    - One-line “Anything you’d tell a friend before riding this?” (hard limit on characters)

This entire flow is designed to:

- Feel finite
- Respect time
- Avoid review fatigue
- Create a complete package


## 5. Longer Written Reviews: Allowed, But De-Emphasised

Long-form text is **permitted but never encouraged**.

Crucially:

- It appears **after the review is already submitted**
- Framed as “Anything else you’d like to note?”
- Has a clear skip button    

This:

- Prevents abandonment
- Makes longer comments feel like a gift, not a task
- Caters to enthusiasts without burdening the majority
- Power users will feel rewarded when filing it out

On the route page:

- Longer notes are collapsed by default
- Always secondary to tags and ride counts

Long text is treated as **context, not signal**. It allows the users who deem it important to express themselves or give clarity on reasoning or narrative behind their rides. This can act almost as a forum post, but never a back and forth dialogue and something that remains in the background.


## 6. What’s Shown on Route Pages (and What Isn’t)

### Prominently shown:

- Times ridden
- Recent activity
- Common tags (conditions, suitability)
- Photos along the route
- Editorial or behavioural signals (“Often re-ridden”, “Saved by guests”)

### Intentionally not shown:

- Public star averages
- “Top” or “Worst” rankings
- Competitive comparisons

This avoids:

- Over-judging routes
- Penalising geography
- Upsetting hosts unfairly

Users self-select using **contextual information**, not scores. The routes aren't there to compete, but to drive sales. We want to present a balanced ecosystem that empowers hosts and guests and makes us money!


## 7. Stars: Collected, Not Displayed

Star ratings exist, but:
- Are used internally (quality signals, surfacing decisions)
- Are not shown as a headline score

Reasons:

- Routes are contextual, not universally “good” or “bad”
- Low scores could unfairly harm hosts
- Tags + behaviour convey suitability far better

If a route is genuinely poor:

- It accumulates caution tags
- Gets fewer re-rides
- Stops surfacing editorially

This creates a natural, non-punitive cooling effect. We aren't punishing anyone through it and the community drives it themselves.


## 8. Hosts vs Users: The Business Reality

A critical framing decision for us to bare in mind:

- **Users** = riders and guests
- **Customers** = hosts

Therefore:

- The system protects hosts from public shaming
- Avoids making routes a liability for them and us
- Still gives riders enough information to choose wisely

Trust with hosts is preserved by:

- No visible low scores
- No harsh judgments
- Emphasis on suitability, not quality


## 9. The Ecosystem Loop

The platform is designed as a self-reinforcing loop:

1. Someone stays
2. They ride nearby routes
3. They tap “I’ve ridden this”
4. They leave light, structured feedback
5. Routes become richer and more trustworthy
6. Routes surface in editorial and property pages
7. New guests book because of routes
8. Cycle repeats

This builds a **living evolving map**, not a static directory. 


## 10. Design Guide

Every feature must pass this question:

> Does this help someone decide whether _this route is right for them_,  
> without judging whether the route is “good”?

If yes → include it  
If it feels judgemental, competitive, or performative → soften or remove it
