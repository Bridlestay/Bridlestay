

## 1. The core differentiation: property as basecamp, not brochure

Unlike Airbnb, where a property page is a static sales brochure for accommodation, **our property listings function as a “basecamp”**.

A stay is not just:

- Where you sleep

It is:

- Where you ride _from_
- How you access land, routes, and local knowledge
- A gateway into the wider horsey community

Every design and integration decision flows from this very idea. We can tell a connected narrative, linking property and purpose in a way other can't.


## 2. Dual storytelling: people and horses are equal

From the very top of the property page, the platform must communicate that it understands **two equally important needs**:

- Human accommodation (beds, kitchen, comfort)
- Horse facilities (stables, turnout, arenas, access)

This is achieved by:

- A **split or dual hero** presentation (“The Stay” vs “The Horses”)
- Curated image sets for each, not one mixed gallery
- Clear labelling so horse facilities are never hidden or secondary

This immediately signals:

> “This platform is built for horse people, not adapted for them.”

If two columns are presented as long cards on the page, we can immediately present a side-by-side comparison, does the horse needs equate my own and are they good enough? Each section aligns nicely, images, description, amenities and facilities etc at a glance.


## 3. Horse facilities as structured, scannable data

Horse facilities are not just shown in photos — they are **explicit, structured signals**:

- Number of stables
- Turnout type
- Arena details
- Secure storage
- Trailer / lorry access
- Direct bridleway or off-road access

These appear **above the fold**, similar to how Airbnb surfaces Wi-Fi or parking, because for horse owners these are non-negotiables.


## 4. Routes as a first-class feature, not an extra add-on

Routes are central to the experience and are surfaced directly on the property page as an important aspect.

### On property listings:

- A **carousel of top routes from this property**
- Each route is tangible:
    - Distance, duration, terrain
    - Reviews        
    - Small map previews


If no routes exist:

- The space is not empty
- It becomes a **prompt to create one**, or to explore the map by guests
- Hosts are encouraged to create routes when listing because:
    - They appear on the listing page
    - They increase booking confidence
	    - They can draw guests in directly from the map page

This helps populate the system early and naturally.


## 5. Community imagery: showing reality, not polish

Beyond host photos, property pages include a **community image strip**:

- Photos pulled from nearby routes
- Labelled clearly as “Ridden from here” / “Seen on local routes”
- Each image links back to:
    - The route it was taken on
    - Its position on that route (if we make it an available option)

This:

- Shows what riding actually looks like around the stay
- Adds authenticity to each page
- Immediately creates a sense of activity and shared usage

Photos become navigational, not decorative. They also drive a sense of community.


## 6. Deep linking between stays, routes, reviews, and photos

Everything links to everything else in meaningful ways:

- Route reviews link back to the stays people booked
- Routes can show:
    - “Ridden by guests staying at…”
- Properties will highlight:
    - Routes guests commonly ride nearby
- Photos show:
    - Where they were taken (Later addition, picking photo location)
    - Who they were taken by
    - Which route they belong to

The user never feels like they’ve “left” something — they are always zooming in or out of the same world. The map becomes the bones of the website, all pages navigate it in some form.


## 7. Behavioural discovery loops (keeps people exploring)

Subtle systems encourage continued exploration:

- “People who rode this also rode…”
- Alternative or complementary routes
- Route-first discovery:
    - View a route → see stays you can ride it from
- Stay-first discovery:
    - View a stay → see rides people actually do from there or nearby

This mirrors proven e-commerce loop patterns without feeling overly commercial, as it focuses on a community driven aspect.


## 8. Horsey identity and community formation

This is where the platform becomes emotionally sticky.

### Profiles are not just for people:

- Optional **horse profiles** in dropdown
    - Name, breed, type that we use from bookings
- Routes show:
    - “Ridden by horses like yours”
- Reviews can reference the horse that rode it
- Images show which profile uploaded the image, but the ability to tag your horse profile means persistent horses throughout the site.

Over time:

- Familiar horses, riders, and properties start to appear repeatedly
- A quiet sense of recognition and community forms

Horsey people feel they get to know certain horses, as they see repeat horse profiles and images, with their riders. This creates a meshing of both human and horse, despite the functions remaining largely the same throughout for us. No other provider does this.


## 9. Local knowledge over generic reviews

Beyond star ratings, the platform surfaces **practical horse-specific wisdom**:

- Seasonal notes (boggy, dry, best time of year)
- Livestock warnings
- Ground conditions

This isn’t opinion — it’s shared stewardship and experience. We are allowing the users to police the route making system and therefore generate content that others use and take part in.


## 10. Post-stay and return-visit intelligence

The system subtly reinforces repeat use via tagging:

- “People often ride this on their second visit” (reinforcing repeat stays)
- “Popular routes after a 3–4 night stay” (reinforcing longer stays)
- Seasonal resurfacing of routes and areas (reasons to book again in the same area)

Progress is tracked softly:

- No loud gamification
- Personal milestones (“first guest stabling stay”, “routes ridden nearby”)

This respects the culture of horse people while still rewarding engagement. Horse people do not want to see game elements. But they will participate in them if they are softly implemented in a discreet way.


## 11. Commercial nudges that feel like confidence, not sales

During booking and planning:

- “Guests usually stay X nights here”
- “Most people ride these routes during their stay”
- “Add this route to your trip”

These reduce uncertainty and decision fatigue rather than pushing upsells. They are gentle reminders and helpful nudges that can help make up their minds, without being too forward. Use the information or don't.


## 12. The unifying philosophy

We are not building:

- A listings site
- A maps tool
- A review platform

We are building:

> **A shared mental map of horse places.**

Every integration answers at least one of:

- Who rides here?
- What does it feel like?
- How do people actually use this place?
- What connects to this?
- What's it like to stay here?

This is the glue that holds the platform together. Hopefully it will be why it will feel fundamentally different from Airbnb, not just more specialised.
